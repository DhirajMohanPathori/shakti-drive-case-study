01 · The Problem

A visible gap between
supply and demand

India's ride-hailing market has a paradox — urban women passengers strongly prefer female drivers, yet fewer than 2% of Ola and Uber drivers are women. It's a supply problem rooted in systemic barriers.

"Driving is a man's job" — deeply ingrained societal beliefs shape platform design, family responses, and what women believe is possible for them.

Synthesized from in-depth interviews with women drivers
01
Safety Concerns
Night rides, isolated routes, and lack of emergency support create anxiety that deters entry.
02
Cultural Stigma
"Driving is masculine" — ingrained beliefs limit women's perception of this as a viable profession.
03
Training Access
Limited women-friendly driving schools, night driving practice, and confidence programs.
04
Family Resistance
Safety and social perception concerns from family create household-level friction.
05
Platform Gaps
No women-specific onboarding, inflexible hours, no childcare support.
02 · Research

Mixed methods,
real voices

126 survey responses + 4 in-depth interviews across women drivers, potential drivers, and passengers. Mixed-method design to balance breadth and depth.

METHOD 01
Quantitative Survey
126 responses from riders, potential drivers, and everyday people. Captured safety perceptions, willingness to drive, and passenger preferences.
METHOD 02
Semi-Structured Interviews
4 in-depth conversations with women drivers and platform managers. Surfaced motivations and lived experiences invisible in survey data.
METHOD 03
Stakeholder Triangulation
Cross-validated findings across drivers, potential drivers, and passengers to find where perceptions diverged.
METHOD 04
Thematic Coding
Systematic qualitative analysis to identify recurring patterns and hypotheses for product intervention.
03 · Insights

What the research
actually revealed

⚠️
Safety x Social Norms Drive Restriction
The intersection of safety concerns and cultural expectations is the primary limiting force. Both must be addressed simultaneously.
👁️
Visibility Creates Normalization
Role models matter more than incentives in early adoption. Showcasing successful women drivers normalizes the occupation.
🏗️
Structured Support Boosts Enrollment
Training programs, financial incentives, and childcare support significantly increase willingness to join.
💰
Customer Demand = Untapped Market Gap
Strong passenger preference for women drivers — including willingness to pay a premium — is a business opportunity platforms are leaving on the table.

Honest Reflection
What worked: Real driver voices gave us grounded hypotheses, not assumptions. Where survey and interview data diverged — those gaps were the most generative.

What didn't: Difficulty finding active women drivers limited sample size. The very problem we were studying made it hard to study.
