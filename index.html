01 · The Problem

A visible gap between
supply and demand

India's ride-hailing market has a paradox — urban women passengers strongly prefer female drivers for safety and comfort, yet fewer than 2% of Ola and Uber drivers are women. This isn't a demand problem. It's a supply problem rooted in systemic barriers.

The question we set out to answer: why do so few women enter ride-hailing as drivers, and what product intervention would actually change that?

"Driving is a man's job" — deeply ingrained societal beliefs don't just shape individual decisions. They shape platform design, family responses, and what women believe is possible for them.

Synthesized from in-depth interviews with women drivers
01
Safety Concerns
Night rides, isolated routes, and lack of emergency support create anxiety that deters entry.
02
Cultural Stigma
"Driving is masculine" — ingrained beliefs limit women's perception of this as a viable profession.
03
Training Access
Limited women-friendly driving schools, night driving practice, and confidence programs.
04
Family Resistance
Safety, social perception, and work-life concerns from family create household-level friction.
05
Platform Gaps
No women-specific onboarding, inflexible hours, no childcare support, missing infrastructure.
02 · Research Approach

Mixed methods,
real voices

We used a mixed-method design combining quantitative survey reach with qualitative interview depth — across three stakeholder groups: current women drivers, potential drivers, and passengers.

METHOD 01
Quantitative Survey
126 responses from riders, potential drivers, and everyday people. Captured breadth of perspectives on safety, willingness to drive, and passenger preferences.
METHOD 02
Semi-Structured Interviews
4 in-depth conversations with women drivers, potential drivers, and platform managers. Surfaced motivations and lived experiences invisible in survey data.
METHOD 03
Stakeholder Triangulation
Cross-validated findings across current drivers, potential drivers, and passengers to surface where perceptions diverged — especially on safety and social norms.
METHOD 04
Thematic Coding
Systematic qualitative analysis across interview transcripts. Identified recurring patterns and emerging hypotheses to guide product intervention.
03 · Key Insights

What the research
actually revealed

Beyond listing barriers, our research surfaced four testable hypotheses that directly shaped the product — each grounded in data, not assumption.

⚠️
Safety x Social Norms Drive Restriction
The intersection of safety concerns and cultural expectations is the primary limiting force — neither alone fully explains low participation. Both must be addressed simultaneously.
👁️
Visibility Creates Normalization
Showcasing successful women drivers shifts perceptions and normalizes the occupation. Role models matter more than incentives in early adoption.
🏗️
Structured Support Boosts Enrollment
Training programs, financial incentives, and childcare support significantly increase willingness to join — structured onboarding unlocks latent supply.
💰
Customer Demand = Untapped Market Gap
Strong passenger preference for women drivers — including willingness to pay a premium — is a clear business opportunity platforms are leaving on the table.

06 · Change Framework

Three levels of
systemic change

ShaktiDrive is designed as a three-level intervention for durable change — not a temporary spike in driver numbers.

LEVEL 01
Individual Empowerment
Give individual women the tools to drive safely and confidently
Safe Route Optimizer builds route confidence progressively
CLM builds skills for night driving, navigation, emergencies
Family Dashboard removes the household veto on driving
LEVEL 02
Platform Innovation
Give platforms the data and incentives to design inclusively
SVDB creates a trust signal visible to platform algorithms
Women-to-women matching unlocks the premium demand segment
Behavioral data on women drivers informs future decisions
LEVEL 03
Ecosystem Change
Shift cultural perception at scale
Visibility of women drivers normalizes the occupation
Community Circles create self-sustaining peer networks
Success stories displace the "driving is masculine" narrative
07 · Measurement

How we'd know
it's working

Success isn't just driver count. We defined a dual measurement framework — tracking quantitative indicators alongside qualitative narrative shifts, because both matter for sustained change.

Quantitative Indicators
% increase in women expressing interest in driving
Perceived safety scores on Likert scale
Passenger comfort levels — male and female
Women driver awareness and discovery rates on platform
Qualitative Indicators
Narrative shifts: hesitation → interest → readiness
Interview insights on confidence and family acceptance
Platform manager and NGO stakeholder reflections
Community perception changes in target neighborhoods

The ultimate signal: a systemic shift from "women rarely drive cabs" to "women driving is normal, safe, and socially supported."

Honest Reflection
What worked: Real driver voices and qualitative depth gave us grounded hypotheses, not assumptions. Where survey and interview data diverged — those gaps were the most generative.

What didn't: Difficulty finding active women drivers — the very problem we were studying made it hard to study. A limitation that would persist into actual product discovery too.
